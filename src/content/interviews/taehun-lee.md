<p>In the summer of 2024, I had the chance to invite the guitarist Taehun Lee — one of the artists I love most — through a residency program in Munich. At the time I was teaching a course at the University of Hildesheim called <em>Reading Uselessness: the Zhuangzi</em>. It seemed to me that the most sensuous way to convey the scene of Cook Ding's blade carving the ox would be to show Taehun improvising, and to hold a conversation with him about improvisation. The result was the most beautiful and memorable class I have ever taught.</p>

<p>The warm sun and cool wind outdoors. Students gathered to sit or lie wherever they liked, listening to the music and asking questions. Birdsong. Taehun taking the birdsong as his motif. And thoughts on freedom unfolding with his playing as their motif.</p>

<p>Through my own busyness and shortcomings, it is only now — two years later, and with the help of artificial intelligence — that I am able to share it. I recommend this conversation to everyone: it offers so many insights into improvisation, freedom, the path, ceaselessness, effort, music, and life.</p>

<p>Once again, through my own shortcomings, I failed to make a proper recording. What does it even mean, I wonder, to record an improvisation whose very essence is to flow away and vanish — and yet I had that wish. Despite it, in the end I could do no more than reconstruct a single improvisation. Even that is of poor sound quality, but I think it at least serves as a document from which one can imagine what took place.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> That improvisation you have just heard was our introduction to our seminar on the Zhuangzi. This is a special occasion for thinking about intercultural philosophy through the medium of contemporary music. I'm Sool, I teach philosophy here. Today we have a very special guest from Korea. I know you hate this, but I wanted to introduce you as one of the most important musicians in Korea right now — and one of my favorites. Welcome, Taehun.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> Hello. Thanks for having me. I'm Taehun, from Seoul, Korea. Nice to meet you all. It's such a beautiful day. Very happy to be here.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> Let's start with some ice-breaking questions. You've been in Germany for about four weeks now. How is your experience so far? How is the food, the weather?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> Food is good. The weather has been crazy, but it's getting better, so I'm happier now. The parks are beautiful — I've been doing a lot of walks. And the bells — church bells. Church bells and the birds, especially in the morning. They are very loud.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> Because there are no birds in Seoul, right?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> There are birds in Seoul, but they're not as loud.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> I heard that on your first or second day in Germany you bought a score of Bach's Chaconne. Is Bach your relationship with Germany as a musician? Or is there something else you like here, musically?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> Bach has always been my champion. You cannot avoid Bach if you study classical music — it's the start of everything. So naturally I got into it early, in high school and college. I still play him a lot, every once in a while, when I feel useless and not good enough. I play Bach and I tell myself, "Oh, you are worthless indeed." And it makes everything better.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> A great philosophical tool. So — you currently play in something like six, seven, eight bands. Your spectrum is very wide: pop, funk, soul, experimental, metal, even traditional crossover. You won the Korean Music Award last year for hip-hop. And one of your latest collaborations was with RM of BTS — you told me that an hour ago and I was like, wow. So you're a kind of celebrity in the Korean indie scene. How do you feel about that?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> I'm just old. You spend enough time in the same place, and everybody gets to know you.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> Okay, maybe we should stop talking and listen to more music. We're here today to talk about improvisation and freedom — maybe even the ascetic and philosophical aspects of improvisation. But let's start by listening to you, and the questions will come up by themselves, or from the audience.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> Let's do it.</p>

<p class="stage">[ Improvisation ]</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> I've realized it's really better to leave the sound wherever it is, and try to listen as much as possible. Because there is always going to be sound somewhere — unless you're in a silent chamber, or in space. I've never been to space yet. But really, that's my goal when I play improvised music. I want to be part of this. I don't want to assert myself any more than the tree and the birds up in the tree do.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> For our course, this is just Daoism in action, right? Doing a kind of nothing. Not thinking. Being one with what's around you.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> And it's the easiest thing, because you don't really have to prepare anything.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> It's easy?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> Listen very hard. Very carefully. That's all you need to do. You practice your instrument — in my case the guitar — just enough to get around, and to convey whatever is going through your ears at that moment, as musically as possible. So I practice a lot, but not in order to play improvised music, not to build something for the improvisation itself. I'm just doing the groundwork. And when a moment like this comes, I just go and have fun.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> It was beautiful. And I'm curious — you're also a composer, you studied composition. Isn't improvisation a kind of anti-composition? How do the two fit together?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> Exactly the opposite. Bach was the greatest improviser the Earth has ever seen — one of the greatest. All his music was improvised before it was written down. Beethoven, Mozart, whoever you name. And not just the old composers — name any of your favorite artists today, they're just coming up with it: playing around the instrument, or hearing something. It's all improvisation. And it has to start from improvisation. Otherwise composition becomes something restricted, trapped inside your head. Never good.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> A great lesson for us philosophers. Stop thinking.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> Stop thinking.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> When you were playing just now, several things were happening — like five different landscapes, five different emotions. There were transitions between very intense feelings. And it began with a single tone, a single note. I'm curious how it begins, where the idea starts, how it flows.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> This one definitely started with that guy up there — the bird, chirping. That became my motif, and I played around with it, with notes that sounded good to me, then notes that worked with the birds and the wind, and I went from there. I try to go to places — different landscapes, as you said. But I don't intend to go to different landscapes anymore, because the moment you intend to go somewhere, you never get there. You have to be there. I just need to be there. I don't know if that makes sense. If you have any questions, you can always just shout.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> I used to think the way you do. Exactly. I was trying to follow my own voice within the improvisation. But once I realized that the sound I'm making — the sound I think I'm making — is the accumulation of everything I've ever listened to, plus whatever is happening at this moment… I feel like the birds, I feel like the trees. I know it sounds too abstract. But if I follow only my own voice, it never gets any fuller than it already is. I feel the essence of improvisation is to find a way to make the sound survive through time. And the most important thing in surviving is not to let the sound perish — to make it fuller, and fuller, as it goes. The only way I've found to do that is to be in the moment: not thinking about my own voice, but going around, really listening to whatever is happening. That has proven helpful. Any other questions?</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> Can you even practice being a good improviser? Or do you just practice the instrument?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> The skill that lets you convey whatever is going through your ears at any given moment is crucial — because once it's gone, it's gone. There are moments when we're not breathing — we are breathing, but there are moments when I'm not inhaling or exhaling. It's like punctuation in writing, where the sentence breathes. My silence does that — it stops, and I have to breathe and recuperate. That's the beauty of it: you're never wrong. All these notes are just different wavelengths. So I focus more on the register. Of course I have a key center, specific notes that make up a key. But when I'm really going, it's more like… I try to imitate the voice. No matter how well-trained a transcriber of music you are, you can never fully capture the transcription of a bird chirping — so many tones and notes at the same time. And you're human, you play wrong notes all the time; you just have to live with it. One easy way I found is not to treat any note as important. The aggregate of the notes matters more than any exact moment. There are important moments, of course, but I always try to defend myself as a maker of mistakes. I'm always drawn to folk tunes — old folk tunes, from my own culture or from other ancient cultures. Korean <em>minyo</em> — those melodies stay with me the most. So whenever I'm lost — and you have to get lost in improvisation, it's very important — these core melodies are the first to come to my rescue. They're like big rubber duckies thrown to me in a moment of crisis. They help, because they're minimal and repetitive, and the intervals are often very strong. They're very effective for navigating the lost stretches of an improvisation.</p>

<p class="q-audience"><strong>Audience:</strong> You mention lost stretches — so even in your improvisation, where there can be no wrong notes, there are stretches where you feel lost. How do you find your way?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> Well, the S-Bahn is always under construction, and you never know where you'll end up. Just like that — I take the S4 into the city center and it never goes the way I expect. So the folk melody works as a map. Or I don't know where the sun is, so I navigate my way out of the station, change to the U-Bahn, and get to wherever I wanted to go in the first place.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> You were disappointed by our public transport here?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> No, it's very, very good. Very nice. Full of adventures. I love adventures.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> Then let's hear another section — or, if anyone has an immediate question, otherwise we'll move to the next improvisation.</p>

<p class="stage">[ Improvisation ]</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> For this kind of improvisation — when she asked me where I come from, somehow I started singing an old New Zealand folk tune. I forget its name, but it's very popular, and popular in Korea too. So I wanted to do something with it. Once that kind of melody starts going in my head, I can't get it out unless I do something about it — which I just did. So this time it wasn't so much about the outside sound; it was more the sound inside my head.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> Does it bother you, having a sound going on in your head?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> Sometimes, because I want to do something awesome.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> You do want to do something awesome, right?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> Sometimes a silly tune just starts playing in my head. I can't get rid of it unless I play it. So I play it like this, with a twist, so people won't quite recognize it — but I do play it. At the end of the day, you just have to play with whatever is coming at you.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> Once someone asked you how to become a better guitar player, and your answer was: you have to sleep with your guitar — fall asleep holding it.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> There's a famous story that John Coltrane took his saxophone to the bathroom — he wouldn't just sleep with it, he'd bathe with it. I'm not that crazy. I only go as far as keeping the guitar by the bed, just in case I wake up early and want to play. But John Coltrane…</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> I wanted to ask you about freedom. Freedom is one of the great themes, also in Daoism. The three albums by your main band, Cadejo, all carry the word "free": <a href="https://music.apple.com/us/album/freesummer/1472470374" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><em>Free Summer</em></a>, <a href="https://music.apple.com/us/album/freebody/1539560020" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><em>Free Body</em></a>, <a href="https://music.apple.com/us/album/freeverse/1695850996" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><em>Free Verse</em></a>. A kind of addiction to freedom.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> Of course. That's all I've ever cared about — freedom.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> So what is freedom in your life? When is it in your music?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> I don't know about Germany, but Korea is a very strict society. When you're little, the moment you enter elementary school, it's almost like joining an army — the standards become so important. That old paradigm is not so strong anymore, but when I was growing up, in the early '90s, it was still there. And from the start, I really didn't like it. So I'd skip school, get caught by my parents, and eventually they sent me out of the country, because I wasn't doing well in the Korean education system. And things got much better — because in America, they don't really care what you do, as long as you do something, as long as you're not harming yourself. Once I felt that kind of liberation, I could never go back. And it's the same in music: everyone has different aesthetics, and mine was never about playing the same thing over and over in the same way. That never appealed to me. So I looked for alternatives, and I found improvisation. It solved most of my problems, as a human and as a musician.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> And yet you still live and work in Korea, in the Korean music industry — a very strictly organized society where expectations of a person are high. How is it to pursue your freedom in Seoul, in this hyper-individualized world?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> Don't tell them it's terrible — because it's fun. I try to drink as much as possible and pretend I don't care; then other people take care of business for me. I'm not kidding. But when I do have to work — work around the system, that is — you spend enough time in an environment, and, well, we're all human: you get used to your surroundings and find your way around eventually. I think I found a kind of compromise. As long as they don't tell me what to do while I'm playing, I'm okay. So I do as much crazy stuff as I want when I'm with my instrument and my band. Otherwise, I behave and act like a normal person. Any questions?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> I used to teach in a music program at a college, and I tried to teach people how to improvise. After many attempts and projects, I realized it's not possible. You can never teach the "how" — because it's like figuring out how to live your own life, which you do by yourself, eventually. Improvisation is just like that: you have to figure it out yourself. I can show my students, my peers, my friends how I do it, but it will never be the same for them. The way I approach and understand ideas — making this sound survive through this stretch of time — will be entirely different when someone else does it. So once I realized that, I just say: "Okay, this is how I do it. You may do it this way, or you may find your own way, but this is how I do it." And I show them. Sometimes it works, sometimes it doesn't. Sometimes it helps immediately, sometimes later in their career. Sometimes I get a call: "Now I think I understand what you meant" — or, "I hate you." Depending on the person. Anyway, "teaching" is such a difficult word for me. There are so many teachers here, I shouldn't say too much about it — but it has never been an easy word for me. So I just try to play more, so that people who are interested can come to my show and see how I improvise. Other than that — if someone comes to me now and asks me to teach them how to improvise, I don't give that lesson anymore.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> When I first went to America, I couldn't speak English at all. My parents said, "You have to go," and I said, "Yes, I'll go" — without knowing what was going on. So the guitar was my only friend. I literally had no friends; I couldn't even manage everyday English. From then on, playing the instrument became a very important part of my life. I took lessons, I went to college to learn more about it. But looking back, it was always about finding the sound I liked on the instrument myself — rather than people telling me what's good and what's bad. So many people do it better than me that way, and it never interested me. It works differently for everyone. Some people need more guidance, more teaching. But for me, it worked better this way. That's why I eventually got stuck with improvising — it's just personality, I think.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> But if it's unteachable, surely there are still typical obstacles that beginners face — blockages on the path to freedom.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> I don't know why you put it in those words, but — the biggest challenge we have as instrumentalists is singing. If you can sing the notes in your head accurately enough, you can play them on an instrument. But instrumentalists hide so much behind their instruments that they forget to sing. This was one of my experiments when teaching improvisation: I'd just make them sing. These guitarists play better than me, they go deep, they vibe all day long, never get tired, never breathe, relentless. But when I make them sing, they can't sing a phrase accurately. I give them a tune I just played — "da da da da da" — and they get it wrong, every time. Behind the instrument it's easy, because it's so automatic: you know where to put your finger, and you get the sound. But you only think you're getting the sound — you're really not, because you're only getting half of it, since in your head you never hear it exactly.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> I think we'd love to hear a song from you — you're talking so much about songs and voices. And maybe you can tell us how it came about: the song, the melody, the words.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> This too is a melody — a song, the melody of a folk tune. I feel like I'm always writing folk music when I write my solo material. This one is from my second solo album, and it's called <em>Graduation</em>. Graduation is such a big, memorable moment in a life — you'll all experience it. I wanted to write a melody, so I listened to a lot of folk music, and one day it just came to me, through a rhyme — like a lullaby. The melody came first; that's usually how it is for me, melody first. And then — I'm really bad with lyrics — so the words and the title come last.</p>

<p class="stage">[ Song — "Graduation" ]</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> Thank you. I'm not sure how much time is left. Actually — now that I think about it — I do prepare, I do practice improvisation. The way I do it, we call it "zoning in." I practice zoning in: a very high, intense level of focus, and I try not to distract myself once I'm in that mode. Once I'm there, I hear better and respond better to the sound. I practice that regularly, every day, at least five or thirty minutes, depending on the day. On a day like today I'm outside the whole time, but that's still working. Being responsive and figuring it out in the moment — the two have to go hand in hand, otherwise you can't keep your focus while responding to the situation. And once you lose focus, it's over — you can't follow through.</p>

<p class="q-audience"><strong>Audience:</strong> Is there any other part of your life where this kind of zoning in happens?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> Sometimes. When I talk to my daughter — she's three — I have to focus even more than when I talk to you, because I can never know where she's coming from, where her motive comes from, what she really wants, what logic lies behind her words. So I really have to focus, and once I zone in to that moment, we click. It doesn't happen often — I fail all the time, I'm a bad father — but once I get into the zone with her, we're fine for a couple of hours, making stupid jokes and silly faces at each other. It becomes natural, because we're both in the zone together.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> This morning you told me a story…</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> …from my musical career — about collaborating with someone I trust musically, and going somewhere with them. Most of my bands work that way now. We only have a general theme, so we're really improvising the bigger structure. I feel free, passing through everybody — they pass through my head too, with drums and bass and whatnot. The organ player taught me a lot, early on, with the blues in high school. That made so much sense, and made me feel I should do this for life. He's probably the one who influenced me the most. In the past, I was the type to just walk up to someone and ask them to play with me. If I saw something at a venue, I'd just go up to them, and if they thought I was crazy — oh well. But most of the time, especially in the Seoul indie scene, which is very small — and it was even smaller twenty years ago — it wasn't hard to reach people and jam. And if we liked what we did, we'd name ourselves a band. That's how I ended up with something like fourteen bands.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> That moment of playing, for me — the feeling that we're talking about the same thing… Improvisation, in essence, is just like conversation. When a conversation goes wrong, we're all talking about different things, saying different things, meaning different things. But when we're really locked in, in the zone, really understanding what we're talking about, we don't even have to say much — we know exactly what we mean. And that becomes a lasting memory you live on, with all the important people in your life. Those are the moments I remember most. When I play, I try to go there, to find that moment — with my colleagues, and even with the audience these days. It's strange, but you can hear the audience from the stage, if you try hard enough — they're always moving, their gestures, their faces, and they make all kinds of sounds. Moments like that are very important.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> To take the second question first — you do notice. But when I play guitar, most of the time my eyes are closed. As I zone in, I'm trying to find myself in a place that doesn't look like this, that isn't three-dimensional. It's hard to explain, but it's as if I'm entering a virtual space. To describe it: I love that King Crimson is playing somewhere in that world, and I'm flying around, or falling into a really deep hole — but I feel fine. It's all my references, my past references, and they build up these walls — like gym walls — and sometimes they're transparent, so I can go from here to there, no problem, however many layers there are. I try to build that musical space here, where I can find a sound I really like at any moment, so I can summon it from my fingers. This is really hard to explain — I might explain it completely differently tomorrow. But that's today's version.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> And the first question was — what was the first question? Sorry. Oh — the conversation. The conversation on the plane. So, they say "good morning," and you say… what would you say if I said good morning to you?</p>

<p class="q-audience"><strong>Audience:</strong> Good morning to you.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> 여기 대중교통에 실망하셨던 거예요?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> 아니요, 아주, 아주 좋아요. 아주 멋져요. 모험으로 가득하죠. 저는 모험을 좋아해요.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> 그럼 다른 구간을 들어보죠 — 바로 떠오르는 질문이 없으시면 다음 즉흥으로 넘어갈게요.</p>

<p class="stage">[ 즉흥연주 ]</p>

<figure>
<img src="/interviews/taehun-lee-2.jpg" alt="눈을 감고 기타 연주에 몰입한 태훈" width="1600" height="900" loading="lazy" />
<figcaption>"기타를 칠 때 저는 대부분 눈을 감고 있어요." — 태훈.</figcaption>
</figure>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> 이런 종류의 즉흥은 — 그녀가 어디서 왔느냐고 물었을 때, 어쩐지 오래된 뉴질랜드 민요를 부르기 시작했어요. 곡 이름은 잊었는데 아주 유명하고, 한국에서도 유명해요. 그래서 그 곡으로 뭔가 해보고 싶었어요. 그런 선율이 머릿속에서 돌기 시작하면, 뭔가 하지 않고는 빠져나오질 못해요 — 방금 그렇게 한 거고요. 그래서 이번엔 바깥 소리라기보다 제 머릿속 소리에 더 가까웠어요.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> 머릿속에서 소리가 돌고 있으면 거슬리지 않으세요?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> 가끔요, 뭔가 멋진 걸 하고 싶거든요.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> 멋진 걸 하고 싶긴 하시는군요, 그렇죠?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> 가끔은 그냥 시시한 곡조가 머릿속에서 재생돼요. 연주하지 않으면 빠져나오질 못하죠. 그래서 이렇게 비틀어서 연주해요. 사람들이 잘 못 알아차리게요 — 그래도 어쨌든 하긴 합니다. 결국엔 그냥 당신에게 다가오는 무엇이든 그걸 가지고 연주해야 하는 거예요.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> 예전에 어떤 사람이 어떻게 하면 더 나은 기타리스트가 되느냐고 물었더니, 답이 "기타랑 같이 자야 해요" — 기타를 안고 잠들어야 한다는 거였다죠.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> 존 콜트레인이 색소폰을 화장실까지 들고 갔다는 유명한 이야기가 있어요. 색소폰을 안고 자는 정도가 아니라 같이 목욕도 했대요. 저는 그 정도로 미치진 않았어요 — 기껏해야 기타를 침대 옆에 두는 정도예요. 아침에 일찍 깨면 혹시 치고 싶을까 봐요. 근데 존 콜트레인은…</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> 자유에 대해 여쭤보고 싶었어요. 자유는 더 큰 주제 중 하나이고, 도교에서도 그렇잖아요. 당신의 메인 밴드 카데호(Cadejo)의 세 앨범이 다 'free'를 달고 있어요 — <a href="https://music.apple.com/us/album/freesummer/1472470374" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><em>Free Summer</em></a>, <a href="https://music.apple.com/us/album/freebody/1539560020" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><em>Free Body</em></a>, <a href="https://music.apple.com/us/album/freeverse/1695850996" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><em>Free Verse</em></a>. 일종의 자유 중독 같은 거죠.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> 당연하죠. 그게 제가 평생 신경 쓰는 전부예요 — 자유요.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> 그럼 당신의 삶에서 자유란 무엇인가요? 음악 속에서는 언제 자유가 있나요?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> 독일은 어떤지 모르겠지만, 한국은 아주 엄격한 사회예요. 어릴 때, 초등학교에 들어가는 순간부터 거의 군대에 들어가는 거나 마찬가지예요 — 기준들이 너무 중요해지죠. 그 낡은 패러다임이 이젠 그렇게까지는 아니지만, 제가 자란 90년대 초만 해도 여전히 그랬어요. 그리고 처음부터 저는 그게 정말 싫었어요. 그래서 학교를 땡땡이쳤고, 부모님께 들켰고, 결국 부모님이 저를 나라 밖으로 내보냈어요. 한국 교육 시스템에서 잘 못 지냈거든요. 그리고 상황이 훨씬 나아졌어요 — 미국에서는 뭘 하든 별로 신경 안 쓰거든요, 뭔가를 하기만 하면, 스스로를 해치지만 않으면요. 그런 해방감을 한번 느끼고 나니 다시는 돌아갈 수 없었어요. 음악에서도 마찬가지예요. 다들 미학이 다르고, 제 미학은 결코 똑같은 걸 똑같은 방식으로 반복하는 게 아니었어요. 그건 제게 매력적이지 않았죠. 그래서 대안을 찾았고, 즉흥을 찾았어요. 그게 인간으로서도, 음악가로서도 제 문제 대부분을 해결해 줬어요.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> 그런데 당신은 여전히 한국에서, 한국 음악 산업 안에서 살고 일하잖아요 — 한 인간에 대한 기대가 높은, 아주 엄격하게 조직된 사회에서요. 이 초개인화된 세상, 서울에서 자유를 좇는다는 건 어떤가요?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> 그들에게 이게 거지같다고 말하진 마세요 — 재밌거든요. 저는 가능한 한 많이 마시고 신경 안 쓰는 척해요. 그러면 다른 사람들이 저 대신 일을 처리해 주거든요. 농담 아니에요. 하지만 정말 일을 해야 할 때는 — 그러니까 시스템 안에서 일하는 거 말이에요 — 어떤 환경에서 충분히 시간을 보내면, 우리 다 인간이잖아요, 결국 주변에 익숙해지고 자기 나름의 길을 찾게 돼요. 저도 일종의 타협점을 찾은 것 같아요. 제가 연주하는 동안 이래라저래라 하지만 않으면 저는 괜찮아요. 그래서 악기와, 밴드와 함께 있을 때는 하고 싶은 만큼 미친 짓을 다 해요. 그 외엔 평범한 사람처럼 처신하죠. 질문 있으신가요?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> 저는 예전에 어느 대학 음악 프로그램에서 가르쳤고, 사람들에게 즉흥하는 법을 가르치려 했어요. 수많은 시도 끝에 그게 불가능하다는 걸 깨달았어요. '어떻게'는 결코 가르칠 수 없어요 — 그건 결국 어떻게 살지를 스스로 알아내는 것과 똑같거든요. 즉흥도 딱 그래요. 스스로 알아내야 하는 거예요. 제 학생들, 동료들, 친구들에게 제가 어떻게 하는지 보여줄 순 있지만, 그들에게는 결코 똑같지 않을 거예요. 제가 아이디어에 접근하고 이해하는 방식 — 이 소리를 이 시간 동안 살아남게 하는 것 — 은 다른 사람이 할 때 완전히 다를 거예요. 그걸 깨닫고 나서는 그냥 이렇게 말해요. "좋아, 난 이렇게 해. 너도 이렇게 하거나 네 길을 찾아도 돼. 하지만 난 이렇게 해." 그리고 보여주죠. 어떨 땐 통하고 어떨 땐 안 통해요. 어떨 땐 즉시, 어떨 땐 나중에 — 그들의 음악 경력에서 도움이 되죠. 가끔 전화가 와요. "이제 무슨 말인지 알 것 같아요" — 아니면 "당신이 미워요." 사람 나름이죠. 어쨌든 '가르친다'는 건 제겐 참 어려운 말이에요. 여기 선생님들이 많아서 너무 많이 말하면 안 되겠지만, 제겐 결코 쉬운 말이 아니었어요. 그래서 그냥 더 많이 연주하려 해요. 관심 있는 분들이 제 공연에 와서 제가 어떻게 즉흥하는지 볼 수 있게요. 그 외엔 — 요즘 누가 즉흥하는 법을 가르쳐 달라고 하면, 저는 그 레슨은 더 이상 안 합니다.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> 처음 미국에 갔을 때 저는 영어를 전혀 못 했어요. 부모님은 "너 가야 해"라고 했고, 저는 "네, 갈게요"라고 했지만 뭐가 어떻게 돌아가는지 몰랐어요. 그래서 기타가 제 유일한 친구였죠. 정말 친구가 하나도 없었고, 일상 영어조차 못 했어요. 그때부터 악기로 연주하는 게 제 삶의 아주 중요한 부분이 됐어요. 레슨도 받고, 악기를 더 배우러 대학도 갔죠. 하지만 돌아보면, 그건 늘 제가 좋아하는 소리를 악기 위에서 스스로 찾으려는 거였어요 — 사람들이 뭐가 좋고 나쁜지 말해주는 것보다요. 그런 식으로는 저보다 잘하는 사람이 너무 많고, 그건 제게 흥미롭지 않았거든요. 사람마다 다르게 작동해요. 어떤 사람은 더 많은 안내가, 가르침이 필요하죠. 하지만 저한테는 이 방식이 더 잘 통했어요. 그래서 결국 즉흥에 붙들리게 된 것 같아요 — 그냥 성향의 문제겠죠.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> 그런데 가르칠 수 없는 거라 해도, 초심자가 겪는 전형적인 장애물 — 자유로 가는 길 위의 막힘 — 은 여전히 있을 것 같은데요.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> 왜 그걸 그런 말로 표현하셨는지 모르겠지만 — 우리가 연주자로서 겪는 가장 큰 도전은 노래 부르기예요. 머릿속의 음들을 충분히 정확하게 부를 수 있으면 악기로 연주할 수 있어요. 그런데 연주자들은 악기 뒤에 너무 숨어서 노래하는 걸 잊어버려요. 이게 제가 즉흥을 가르칠 때 했던 실험 중 하나였어요 — 그냥 노래를 시켰죠. 이 기타리스트들, 저보다 잘 치고 하루 종일 깊이 빠져들고 미친 듯이 하고 절대 안 지치고 숨도 안 쉬고 끈질겨요. 그런데 노래를 시키면 한 프레이즈조차 정확하게 부르질 못해요. 제가 방금 친 곡조를 줘요 — "다 다 다 다 다" — 그러면 매번 틀려요. 악기 뒤에서는 쉬워요. 너무 자동적이거든요 — 손가락을 어디 둘지 알면 소리가 나니까요. 하지만 소리를 낸다고 생각만 하는 거예요. 사실은 아니에요. 절반밖에 못 내고 있는 거죠 — 머릿속에서 결코 정확하게 듣고 있지 않으니까요.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> 당신의 노래를 한 곡 듣고 싶어요 — 노래와 목소리 얘기를 이렇게 많이 하시니까요. 그게 어떻게 만들어졌는지 — 노래든 선율이든 가사든 — 말씀해 주셔도 좋고요.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> 이것도 선율 — 노래예요, 민요의 선율이죠. 저는 솔로 작업을 할 때 늘 민요를 쓰고 있는 것 같아요. 이 곡은 두 번째 솔로 앨범에 실렸고 제목은 <em>Graduation(졸업)</em>이에요. 졸업은 인생에서 정말 크고 기억에 남는 순간이잖아요 — 여러분도 다 겪게 될 거고요. 선율을 하나 쓰고 싶어서 민요를 많이 들었는데, 어느 날 그냥 운율을 통해 떠올랐어요 — 자장가처럼요. 선율이 먼저 왔어요. 저는 보통 그래요, 선율이 먼저예요. 그리고 — 가사를 정말 못 써서 — 가사랑 제목은 맨 마지막에 나와요.</p>

<p class="stage">[ 노래 — 「Graduation」 ]</p>

<figure>
<img src="/interviews/taehun-lee-3.jpg" alt="기타 넥을 살짝 든 채 연주하는 태훈" width="1600" height="900" loading="lazy" />
<figcaption>힐데스하임, 2024년 6월. 위 영상의 스틸 컷.</figcaption>
</figure>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> 감사합니다. 시간이 얼마나 남았는지 모르겠네요. 사실 — 지금 생각해 보니 — 저도 준비를 하고, 즉흥을 연습해요. 그걸 우리는 '존(zone)에 들어간다'고 불러요. 저는 존에 들어가는 걸 연습해요. 아주 높고 강렬한 집중의 정도인데, 그 모드에 있을 땐 제 자신을 흐트러뜨리지 않으려 해요. 그 모드에 들어가면 더 잘 듣고, 소리에 더 잘 반응하죠. 그걸 규칙적으로, 매일 적어도 5분, 30분씩, 날에 따라 연습해요. 오늘 같은 날엔 하루 종일 밖에 있지만 그래도 그게 일이거든요. 반응하는 것과 그 순간에 알아내는 것 — 이 둘은 손에 손을 잡고 가야 해요. 그렇지 않으면 상황에 반응하면서 집중을 유지할 수가 없어요. 한번 집중을 잃으면 다 끝이에요 — 끝까지 따라갈 수가 없죠.</p>

<p class="q-audience"><strong>청중:</strong> 삶의 다른 부분에서도 이런 식으로 존에 들어가는 일이 있나요?</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> 가끔요. 제 딸이랑 이야기할 때 — 세 살인데 — 여러분과 이야기할 때보다 더 집중해야 해요. 그 애가 어디서 왔는지, 동기가 어디서 나왔는지, 정말 뭘 원하는지, 말 뒤에 어떤 논리가 있는지 결코 알 수 없거든요. 그래서 정말 집중해야 하고, 일단 그 순간으로 존에 들어가면 우리는 통해요. 자주 일어나진 않아요 — 저는 늘 실패해요, 나쁜 아빠예요 — 하지만 한번 그 애와 존에 들어가면 몇 시간 동안 괜찮아요. 서로에게 바보 같은 농담을 하고 우스꽝스러운 표정을 짓죠. 그게 자연스러워져요. 우리 둘 다 함께 존 안에 있으니까요.</p>

<p class="q-sool"><strong>Sool:</strong> 오늘 아침에 제게 이야기 하나를 해주셨죠…</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> …제 음악 경력에서요 — 음악적으로 신뢰하는 누군가와 협업해서 함께 어딘가로 가는 거요. 요즘 제 밴드들 대부분이 그렇게 해요. 큰 틀의 주제만 있어서 더 큰 구조를 즉흥으로 하는 거죠. 모두를 스쳐 지나가며 자유롭게 느껴요 — 그들도 드럼이랑 베이스랑 뭐 그런 것들과 함께 제 머릿속을 지나가죠. 오르간 연주자가 고등학교 때 일찍부터 블루스로 제게 정말 많은 걸 가르쳐 줬어요. 그게 정말 말이 됐고, 이걸 평생 해야겠다고 느끼게 해줬어요. 아마 그가 제게 가장 큰 영향을 준 사람일 거예요. 예전엔 저는 그냥 누군가에게 다가가 같이 연주하자고 청하는 쪽이었어요. 공연장에서 뭔가 보이면 그냥 다가갔죠. 절 미쳤다고 생각하면 뭐 어쩔 수 없고요. 하지만 대부분, 특히 아주 좁은 서울 인디 씬에서는 — 20년 전엔 더 좁았고요 — 사람들에게 다가가 잼하는 게 어렵지 않았어요. 그리고 한 게 마음에 들면 우리 스스로를 밴드라고 이름 붙였죠. 그렇게 밴드가 한 14개쯤 됐어요.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> 연주할 때 그 순간이 제겐 — 우리가 같은 것에 대해 이야기하고 있다는 그 느낌이… 즉흥은 본질적으로 대화와 같아요. 대화가 잘못되면 우리는 다 다른 것을 이야기하고, 다른 것을 말하고, 다른 것을 의미하죠. 하지만 우리 모두가 정말로 맞물려, 존 안에서, 무슨 이야기를 하는지 정말 이해하면, 굳이 많이 말할 필요도 없어요. 정확히 아니까요. 그리고 그게 인생의 그 모든 소중한 사람들과 함께 살아가며 간직하는 오래 남는 기억이 돼요. 그 순간들이 제가 가장 많이 기억하는 순간들이에요. 연주할 때 저는 거기로 가려고, 그 순간을 찾으려고 해요 — 동료들과, 요즘엔 청중과도요. 이상하게 들리지만, 정말 열심히 하면 무대 위에서 청중을 어느 정도 들을 수 있어요 — 늘 움직이고, 몸짓과 표정, 온갖 소리를 내거든요. 그런 순간들이 아주 중요해요.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> 두 번째 질문부터 답하자면 — 당신은 알아차려요. 하지만 기타를 칠 때 저는 대부분 눈을 감고 있어요. 존에 들어가면서, 이렇게 보이지 않는, 3차원이 아닌 어떤 장소에서 제 자신을 찾으려 하죠. 설명하기 어렵지만, 마치 가상의 공간으로 들어가는 것 같아요. 그 장소를 묘사하자면 — 킹 크림슨이 그 세계 어딘가에서 연주하는 게 정말 좋고, 저는 그 주위를 날아다니거나 아주 깊은 구멍으로 떨어지는데 — 그래도 괜찮아요. 그건 다 제 레퍼런스들, 과거의 레퍼런스들이에요. 그것들이 체육관 벽 같은 벽들을 쌓는데, 가끔은 투명해서 층이 아무리 많아도 여기서 저기로 문제없이 갈 수 있어요. 저는 그 음악적 공간을 여기에 지으려 해요. 어느 순간에든 정말 좋아하는 소리를 찾을 수 있는 곳을요. 그래서 손가락에서 그걸 불러낼 수 있죠. 이건 정말 설명하기 어려워요 — 내일이면 완전히 다르게 설명할지도 몰라요. 하지만 그게 오늘의 버전이에요.</p>

<p class="a-taehun"><strong>Taehun:</strong> 그리고 첫 번째 질문은 — 첫 번째 질문이 뭐였죠? 죄송해요. 아 — 그 대화. 비행기에서의 대화요. 그래서 그들이 "굿모닝"이라고 하면, 당신은 뭐라고… 제가 굿모닝이라고 하면 뭐라고 답하시겠어요?</p>
